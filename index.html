Section 01 — Foundation

What is a
Pre-Mortem?

The discipline of imagining failure before it happens — and building better plans because of it.

The Core Idea

A pre-mortem is a structured group exercise run before a project begins (or at a critical decision point). Participants are asked to imagine that the project has already failed — then work backwards to identify what caused that failure.

The technique was developed by psychologist Gary Klein and popularised by Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman. It works because it gives people permission to voice doubts they might otherwise suppress.

In a standard planning meeting, people stay positive and go along with the group. In a pre-mortem, pessimism is the task. That shift in framing is what unlocks honest, useful information.

The Key Insight

"If we assume failure, we're not being negative — we're being thorough."

What it is NOT

  • A blame session or performance review
  • A reason to not proceed with a project
  • A risk register (though it informs one)
  • A meeting where the loudest voice wins
Why it Works — The Science

Prospective Hindsight

Imagining an event has already happened increases your ability to identify reasons for it by up to 30%. The brain reasons better in the past tense than the future tense.

Override Optimism Bias

Teams naturally underestimate how long things take and overestimate how smoothly they'll go. The pre-mortem format structurally counters this bias by making the question "what went wrong?"

Unlock Silent Knowledge

Junior team members, cross-functional partners, and quieter personalities often see risks that leaders miss. Pre-mortems give those voices a structured, legitimised way in.

When to Run One

✓ Good Moments

  • At project kick-off, before execution begins
  • Before a major milestone decision (go/no-go)
  • When taking on a new product category or market
  • Before a key external commitment (launch date, partner reveal)
  • When the team has a nagging feeling something might go wrong

✗ Less Useful Moments

  • Mid-execution when the plan is largely locked
  • After a crisis has already started (use post-mortem instead)
  • When the decision has already been made and won't change
  • With a group too small to generate diverse perspectives (under 4 people)

For Operational Excellence facilitators: The pre-mortem is one of the highest-leverage tools you can bring to a product team. It surfaces Uncertainty Debt early — the unacknowledged assumptions and dependencies that cause critical path failure downstream. Run it at kick-off and you compress the decision-making cycle for everything that follows.

Section 02 — Process

The Facilitator
Guide

A complete step-by-step playbook for running a pre-mortem with any product team.

Session Overview
Setup5 min
Imagine Failure5 min
Surface Risks15 min
Prioritise10 min
Mitigate10 min
Commit5 min

Total: 45–60 minutes. Can be compressed to 30 min for experienced groups or expanded to 90 min for complex projects with large teams.

Before the Session
P

Preparation (24–48 hrs before)

Pre-session

Set context. Send participants a 3-sentence brief: what the project is, what the session will do, and what you need from them mentally. Do NOT send a detailed agenda — you want unfiltered instincts, not prepared answers.

Invite the right people. Aim for 6–12 participants. Include at least one person per function that touches the critical path. Cross-functional diversity is the point — homogenous groups produce homogenous blind spots.

Avoid inviting only enthusiastic advocates of the project. You need people with legitimate concerns and real stake in the outcome. If senior leadership is present, brief them beforehand: their job is to listen, not lead.
During the Session
1

Frame the Session

5 minutes

Open by stating clearly: this is not a brainstorm. You are not here to solve problems — you are here to find them. Briefly explain the pre-mortem method and why it works. Then state the premise:

"It is now [12 months from today]. This project has failed — completely, visibly, and painfully. The launch missed, the business didn't land, or the team fell apart. Your job in the next few minutes is to write down every reason you can think of for why that happened."
Adapt the timeframe to project length — use 6 months for a short initiative or 18 months for a multi-season programme. Specificity makes the imagined failure feel real, which is what drives useful output.
2

Silent Individual Writing

5 minutes

Everyone writes independently and silently. No talking, no sharing yet. Each person writes every failure reason they can think of — no filter, no editing, no self-censoring.

Give each participant their own copy of the session template (Section 03). Ask them to aim for a minimum of 5 failure reasons. More is better at this stage.

Silence is critical here. The moment people start talking, they start anchoring on each other's ideas. The independent writing phase is what generates the diversity of perspective that makes pre-mortems valuable. Protect it.
3

Round-Robin Sharing

15 minutes

Go around the room one person at a time. Each person shares one failure reason per turn. Capture all responses on a shared surface (whiteboard, Miro, or shared doc). Continue rotating until all unique reasons are on the board.

Your job as facilitator is to capture, clarify, and prevent discussion. If someone tries to explain or debate a risk, say: "Great — we'll come back to that. Next person."

  • Cluster similar items as you go — create natural categories (people, process, external, technical, commercial)
  • Ask brief clarifying questions only when a reason is genuinely unclear
  • If someone says "I agree with what X said" — push them: "what's your version of that risk?"
Watch for cascade silence — when everyone has shared "their main risk" and energy drops. Push one more round. The second-tier risks are often the most honest and the most overlooked.
4

Prioritise the Risks

10 minutes

With all risks visible, ask the group to vote on the ones they consider highest risk. A simple method: give everyone 5 votes to allocate however they choose across the risk list (dot voting works well in person; a simple show of hands works for smaller groups).

After voting, you should have a clear top 5–7 risks that warrant attention. These are your Priority Risk Register.

Don't force consensus on the ranking. If one risk gets disproportionate votes, that's a signal. If opinions are very spread, that itself is useful data about uncertainty. Name it.
5

Mitigation Discussion

10 minutes

Take the top 3–5 risks only. For each, ask: "What would have to be true for this risk NOT to materialise?" Then: "Who owns the action to get us there?"

You are not trying to solve every risk in the room. You are identifying the actions that change the project plan or require a decision before you proceed.

  • Each mitigation needs an owner and a deadline — not just a note
  • Some risks may be unmitigable — name them as accepted risks
  • Some risks may reveal that a plan assumption needs to change entirely
The most powerful outcome is not a long to-do list. It's one or two decisions that fundamentally improve the plan. Be willing to say "this risk tells us we need to revisit our go-date."
6

Close and Commit

5 minutes

Summarise what happened in the session: the number of risks surfaced, the top priorities identified, and the actions committed. Thank the group — especially anyone who voiced an uncomfortable truth.

State clearly what happens next: who captures the output, when it will be shared, and how it feeds into the project plan or critical path.

Send a summary within 24 hours. Pre-mortem outputs that don't make it into the working plan within a week are forgotten. Your job doesn't end when the meeting does.

Section 04 — Reference

Quick Reference
Card

Print and keep. One page you can bring into any session.

Pre-Mortem — At a Glance
Imagine the failure. Surface the risks. Strengthen the plan.

The 6 Steps

01Frame the session — set the failure premise
02Silent individual writing (5 min, no talking)
03Round-robin sharing — one risk per turn
04Dot-vote to prioritise top risks
05Discuss mitigations for top 3–5 risks
06Assign owners, close, send summary in 24hrs

The Facilitator's Job

→Protect the silence in step 2
→Prevent debate during step 3 — capture, don't solve
→Push for a second round of risks — second-tier is often better
→Name risks that surface as plan assumptions
→Focus mitigation on the top 3–5 only
→Every mitigation needs an owner + deadline

Key Phrases to Use

"It is now [date]. The project has failed. Why?
"What's your version of that risk?
"Great — we'll come back to that. Next person.
"What would have to be true for this NOT to happen?
"Who owns this? By when?
"Is this a risk we're mitigating or accepting?

Warning Signs Mid-Session

!Group converging too fast — push more rounds
!Debate breaking out — redirect to capture
!Only senior voices contributing — explicitly invite quieter members
!Risks vague / high-level — ask "what specifically?"
!No one named the obvious risk — sometimes you have to model it

Timing Guide

Standard session: 45–60 min
Compressed: 30 min
Complex / large team: 90 min
Ideal group size: 6–12 people

What Good Output Looks Like

15–25 distinct risks surfaced. Top 5 prioritised and voted. 3–5 clear mitigations with owners. 1–2 plan changes triggered.

What to Do After

  • Send summary within 24 hours
  • Integrate mitigations into CP
  • Flag accepted risks to leadership
  • Schedule a check-in at midpoint
Section 05 — Support

Frequently Asked
Questions

What your team will ask before and after their first session.

Before the Session
The pre-mortem is one of the most beginner-friendly facilitation tools precisely because the structure does most of the work. Your job isn't to be a trained coach — it's to protect the process. Read the guide, practice the key phrases, and trust the format. If you follow the 6 steps, the room will do the rest. Your first session doesn't need to be perfect; it needs to happen.
Don't oversell it. Say: "I want to run a 45-minute session with you before kick-off to stress-test the plan. It's structured, fast, and has a good track record of surfacing things we'd otherwise find out too late." Most teams respond well when they understand it protects the project, not interrogates it. If a team lead is resistant, ask them: "Is there anything about this plan you haven't had a chance to voice yet?" That question usually opens the door.
Include at least one person from each function that sits on the critical path — design, development, sourcing, commercial, marketing, operations depending on project type. Include someone who is new to the project, someone who has worked on a similar project before, and if possible, someone who disagreed with a key assumption in the plan. Avoid having only the project team's closest advocates. Senior leaders should be present only if they can commit to listening mode.
During the Session
First — protect the silent writing phase. If people write independently first, they'll always have something to share. Second, if the round-robin stalls, use category prompts: "What about external dependencies? What about the team's capacity? What about the assumptions baked into the timeline?" These tend to unlock a second wave. If genuine silence persists, it may mean the group hasn't internalised the failure premise. Restate it more vividly: "Imagine the Monday morning where you have to explain to leadership what went wrong. What are you saying?"
This is the most common failure mode for pre-mortems. Mitigate it in three ways: (1) Brief senior attendees beforehand that their role is to listen and learn, not lead. (2) Enforce the round-robin — go around the room in order, so no one person can dominate the floor. (3) After the senior person speaks, explicitly invite quieter voices: "I'd love to hear from someone who hasn't spoken yet." If the senior person is the project sponsor, consider whether they should attend at all — their presence can suppress honest input.
This is natural — smart people instinctively want to fix problems. Your job is to redirect it: "Really useful — let's park that in the mitigation phase. Who else has a risk to add?" Keep a "parking lot" column on your capture sheet for solution ideas that come up too early. People find it easier to hold their idea when they know it won't be lost. Debate before all risks are surfaced will narrow the list prematurely — protect the generation phase first.
After the Session
Keep it simple: a one-page summary with (1) the top 5 risks ranked by vote, (2) the mitigation action and owner for each, and (3) any plan changes triggered by the session. Send it as a shared doc or PDF within 24 hours. Don't bury it in a long write-up. The goal is for every participant to be able to read it in under 90 seconds and know what they own.
Integrate them into the project's critical path or project management tool — not as a separate risk log that nobody reads. Each mitigation should have a named owner, a deadline, and a checkpoint where it's reviewed. Build a 15-minute pre-mortem review into the next planning milestone of the project. Ask: "Of the risks we surfaced, which are now resolved, which are live, and which need escalation?" Ownership is the variable that separates pre-mortems that create change from pre-mortems that feel good in the moment.
Yes — for longer projects (6+ months), consider a mid-point check. By then, the original risk landscape has shifted: some risks have materialised, some have resolved, and new ones have emerged that weren't visible at kick-off. A 30-minute refresher using the same format — "imagine it's failed, why?" — can surface the risks that belong to the execution phase, not the planning phase. Call it a mid-mortem if that helps distinguish it from the original session.

Have a question not covered here? The best way to build capability is to run sessions and debrief them. After your first pre-mortem, ask yourself: what worked, what felt awkward, and what would you do differently? That reflection is how you build a facilitation practice.
